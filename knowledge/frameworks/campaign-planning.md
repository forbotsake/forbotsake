# Campaign Structure Checklist

How to plan, execute, and measure a marketing campaign without dropping balls.

## Pre-Launch (Week Before)

Everything before you publish a single thing. Skip this and you'll spend launch week scrambling instead of shipping.

### Positioning Locked

- [ ] Positioning statement written and reviewed (see positioning-canvas.md)
- [ ] ICP defined with all 5 persona questions answered (see audience-segmentation.md)
- [ ] Can explain the product in one sentence that makes ICP stop scrolling
- [ ] Messaging pillars defined (3 max) with proof points for each
- [ ] "Would your ICP forward this?" test passed

### Assets Ready

- [ ] Landing page or product page live and working
- [ ] Demo, screenshot, or GIF of the product in action
- [ ] Social profiles updated (bio, link, pinned post)
- [ ] Content pieces written, reviewed, and formatted for each platform
- [ ] CTA links tested (sign up, trial, demo request, whatever the ask is)
- [ ] Tracking in place (analytics on the landing page, UTM parameters for links)

### Channels Selected

- [ ] Top 2 channels scored using the 5-factor rubric (see channel-selection.md)
- [ ] Content formatted natively for each channel (not copy-pasted)
- [ ] Posting schedule defined (what day, what time, what piece)
- [ ] Engagement plan ready (who to tag, what communities to share in, how to respond to comments)

### Success Criteria Defined

- [ ] Primary metric chosen (signups, clicks, replies, revenue — pick ONE)
- [ ] "Keep going" threshold defined (what number after 2 weeks means the channel works)
- [ ] "Pivot" threshold defined (what number means try channel #3)
- [ ] Baseline captured (current numbers before the campaign)
- [ ] Measurement method confirmed (where to check, how often)

## Launch Week (Days 1-7)

The campaign is live. Execute the plan. Don't improvise the strategy mid-week — that's next week's job.

### Day 1: Ship the First Piece

- [ ] Publish the anchor content (your strongest piece, on your primary channel)
- [ ] Share it in 2-3 relevant communities (with context, not just a link drop)
- [ ] Engage with every comment/reply within 4 hours
- [ ] Note initial reaction: what's the immediate response?
- [ ] Cross-post a shortened version to secondary channel if applicable

### Day 2: Engage and Amplify

- [ ] Respond to all comments from Day 1
- [ ] Find 3-5 conversations happening about your problem (on any platform) and add genuine value. Don't pitch. Help.
- [ ] If Day 1 content got traction, quote-tweet/reshare with a new angle
- [ ] If Day 1 content flopped, note what you think went wrong but don't panic — one data point isn't a trend

### Day 3: Second Piece

- [ ] Publish the second content piece (different angle on the same topic)
- [ ] Test a different format or hook style to see what resonates
- [ ] Continue engaging with Day 1 and Day 2 conversations
- [ ] Check metrics: any signal yet?

### Day 4: Listen Day

- [ ] Don't publish anything
- [ ] Read every comment, reply, and mention from the week so far
- [ ] Look for patterns: what questions do people ask? What resonates? What falls flat?
- [ ] Search for your product name, your problem keywords, and competitor names
- [ ] Save notable quotes, questions, and feedback — these become future content

### Day 5: Third Piece (Informed by Feedback)

- [ ] Publish the third piece, adjusted based on what you learned on Day 4
- [ ] Address the most common question or objection you've seen
- [ ] This piece should feel more natural than Day 1 — you're learning the audience voice
- [ ] Engage with all responses

### Days 6-7: Weekend Check

- [ ] Light engagement only (respond to replies, don't force new content)
- [ ] Draft quick notes on what's working and what isn't
- [ ] Prepare for Week 2 adjustments
- [ ] Don't check metrics obsessively — wait for the weekly review

## Post-Launch (Week 2 and Beyond)

The real work starts after the initial push. Most campaigns die here because founders declare victory or defeat too early.

### Week 2: Adjust and Continue

- [ ] Review Week 1 metrics against success criteria
- [ ] What content performed best? Why? (Topic? Format? Time? Platform?)
- [ ] What flopped? Why? (Wrong audience? Weak hook? Bad timing?)
- [ ] Adjust the content plan for Week 2 based on learnings
- [ ] Publish 2-3 more pieces with the adjustments applied
- [ ] Continue engagement (this is not optional — engagement drives algorithmic reach)

### End of Week 2: Decision Point

- [ ] Run /forbotsake-retro for a full analysis
- [ ] Compare results against pre-defined success criteria
- [ ] Make the call:
  - **KEEP GOING:** Numbers meet or exceed the "keep going" threshold. Double down on what's working.
  - **ADJUST:** Some signal but not enough. Change the content angle, timing, or format. Give it 2 more weeks.
  - **PIVOT:** Below the "pivot" threshold with no signal. Move to channel #3 and reset the 2-week experiment.

### Ongoing (If Keeping the Channel)

- [ ] Maintain consistent publishing cadence (don't go from 3x/week to 1x/month)
- [ ] Build a content backlog so you're never scrambling
- [ ] Track metrics weekly, not daily (daily metrics create panic, weekly metrics create insight)
- [ ] Run /forbotsake-retro every 2 weeks
- [ ] Update strategy.md quarterly with what you've learned

## Common Campaign Mistakes

### 1. No Measurement Plan

**What it looks like:** "We'll publish some content and see what happens."

**Why it fails:** Without a specific metric and a specific threshold, you can't make the keep/pivot decision. You'll either keep doing something that isn't working (because there's no trigger to stop) or stop something that IS working (because you don't know it's working).

**Fix:** Before publishing anything, write down: "If we get X [metric] in 2 weeks, we continue. If we get less than Y, we pivot." Put it in strategy.md so you can't move the goalposts.

### 2. Too Many Channels at Once

**What it looks like:** "We're launching on X, LinkedIn, Reddit, HN, Product Hunt, and our blog simultaneously."

**Why it fails:** You do all of them at 20% effort. None of them get enough content, enough engagement, or enough consistency to work. You burn out in 2 weeks and conclude "marketing doesn't work for us."

**Fix:** Two channels. Maximum. One primary, one secondary. Put 70% of your effort into the primary. Give it 2 full weeks before evaluating.

### 3. No Follow-Up

**What it looks like:** A burst of content in Week 1, then silence.

**Why it fails:** Marketing compounds. The first 10 tweets build your voice. The next 10 build your audience. The next 10 build your authority. If you stop at 10, you've invested the effort without capturing the compounding return.

**Fix:** Plan for 4 weeks minimum, not 1 week. If you can't sustain 4 weeks of content on a channel, choose a lower-effort channel.

### 4. Optimizing for Vanity Metrics

**What it looks like:** "Our tweet got 50,000 impressions!" But zero signups.

**Why it fails:** Impressions, likes, and follower count feel good but don't pay bills. A tweet with 500 impressions and 5 signups is infinitely more valuable than a tweet with 50,000 impressions and 0 signups.

**Fix:** Track the metric that matters for your business. Usually it's one of: signups, trial starts, demo requests, or revenue. Everything else is a leading indicator at best.

### 5. Changing the Message Every Day

**What it looks like:** Monday you're a "developer productivity tool," Wednesday you're an "AI-powered code assistant," Friday you're a "team collaboration platform."

**Why it fails:** Repetition creates recognition. Your ICP needs to see the same core message 5-7 times before it sticks. If you change the message daily, they see 7 different products and remember none of them.

**Fix:** Lock your positioning for the duration of the experiment (2 weeks minimum). You can test different hooks and angles, but the core message stays the same.

### 6. All Broadcast, No Conversation

**What it looks like:** You publish content but never reply to comments, never engage in discussions, never DM people who showed interest.

**Why it fails:** Social channels reward conversation. The algorithm surfaces content that generates replies. People buy from people they've talked to, not people who talked at them.

**Fix:** For every hour spent creating content, spend 30 minutes engaging. Reply to every comment. DM people who share your content. Join conversations where your expertise is relevant. The engagement IS the marketing.

### 7. Launching Without a Landing Page

**What it looks like:** Your tweet goes viral but the link in your bio goes to a GitHub repo with no README.

**Why it fails:** You've got their attention for 30 seconds. If there's no clear page that explains the product and has a CTA, that attention evaporates.

**Fix:** Before publishing any content, make sure the link destination is ready. It doesn't need to be beautiful. It needs to be clear: what is this, who is it for, and what should I do next.

### 8. Comparing Day 1 to Someone's Year 3

**What it looks like:** "My tweet got 12 likes but [well-known person] gets 500 on everything."

**Why it fails:** You're comparing your starting line to their current position. They had 12-like tweets too, 3 years ago. Growth is exponential, not linear, and the beginning is always slow.

**Fix:** Compare yourself to yourself last week. Not to anyone else. If Week 2 is better than Week 1, you're on track.
