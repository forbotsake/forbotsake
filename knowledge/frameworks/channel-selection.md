# Channel-Market Fit Scoring Rubric

How to pick the right channels. Not all of them. The right ones.

## The 5 Scoring Factors

Score each channel 1-10 on each factor. Multiply, don't add. A channel that scores 10/10/10/10/1 is worse than one that scores 7/7/7/7/7. One weak factor kills the whole channel.

### Factor 1: Audience Presence

Does your ICP actually spend time here? Not "could they be here" but "do they open this app daily?"

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1-3 | Your ICP rarely or never uses this channel. You'd be shouting into an empty room. |
| 4-6 | Your ICP is present but passive. They scroll but don't engage deeply. Or they use it but not for professional/product discovery. |
| 7-8 | Your ICP actively uses this channel for professional content. They follow accounts, read threads, engage in discussions related to your problem space. |
| 9-10 | Your ICP lives here. This is their primary channel for discovering tools, learning, and discussing the problems you solve. You can name 10 specific people in your ICP who are active here. |

### Factor 2: Content-Format Fit

Does the channel's native format match the kind of content your product needs?

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1-3 | The format actively works against you. Your product needs long-form explanation but the channel is short-form. Or your product is visual but the channel is text-only. |
| 4-6 | You can make it work but it's awkward. You're fighting the format instead of leveraging it. |
| 7-8 | The format fits naturally. Your key message can be communicated well in this channel's native format. |
| 9-10 | The format is a perfect match. The channel was basically designed for the type of content your product story requires. (Example: a CLI tool demo fits perfectly in a short video or GIF-heavy tweet thread.) |

### Factor 3: Founder Capability

Can YOU create content here, right now, without hiring anyone?

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1-3 | You have no presence, no skill, and no interest in creating content for this channel. You'd need to hire someone or spend months building the skill. |
| 4-6 | You could do it but it would be a stretch. You'd be learning the format while trying to market. |
| 7-8 | You're comfortable here. You consume content on this channel regularly and understand what works. You could start producing within a week. |
| 9-10 | You're already active here. You have an audience (even small), you understand the norms, and you enjoy creating content in this format. |

### Factor 4: Cost

What does it cost in time and money per piece of content?

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1-3 | High cost per piece. Each piece requires significant production (video editing, design work, paid promotion to get any reach). Over 4 hours per piece. |
| 4-6 | Moderate cost. 1-4 hours per piece including creation, formatting, and distribution. Some tools or subscriptions needed. |
| 7-8 | Low cost. Under 1 hour per piece. Minimal tools needed. You can batch-create content efficiently. |
| 9-10 | Near-zero marginal cost. You can create and publish in under 30 minutes. The content creation process itself generates ideas for next pieces. |

### Factor 5: Time-to-Signal

How fast can you tell if this channel is working?

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1-3 | Months before you know. SEO, YouTube, and podcast channels can take 3-6 months to show any signal. You're investing blind. |
| 4-6 | Weeks. You'll have some data after 2-4 weeks of consistent posting, but the signal is noisy. |
| 7-8 | Days. You get engagement data within 24-48 hours. You can iterate on messaging weekly. |
| 9-10 | Hours. Real-time feedback. You post, you see reactions, you adjust. The feedback loop is tight enough to run multiple experiments per week. |

## How to Score

1. For each channel, score all 5 factors independently
2. Multiply all 5 scores together to get a composite score
3. Rank channels by composite score
4. Pick the top 2. Ignore everything else until those 2 are working
5. Re-score monthly as your capability and audience evolve

**Minimum viable score:** If any single factor is below 3, eliminate the channel regardless of the composite. A channel where your ICP isn't present (factor 1 = 2) can't be saved by being cheap and fast.

## Channel-Specific Notes

### X/Twitter

- **Strengths:** Fast feedback loop (hours). Low cost per piece. Great for technical audiences and developer tools. Thread format works well for "here's what I learned building X" content. Retweets provide organic reach beyond your follower count.
- **Weaknesses:** Algorithmic feed means you need hooks. High noise floor. Reach is zero with no followers unless you get engagement early (first 30 minutes matter). Short shelf life (24-48 hours).
- **Best for:** Developer tools, technical content, hot takes, build-in-public narratives, engaging with other builders.
- **Content that works:** Threads with a specific insight in tweet 1. Screenshots/GIFs of the product. Contrarian takes backed by evidence. "I just shipped X, here's what I learned."
- **Founder capability note:** If you're already on X and understand the culture, score high. If you've never tweeted, the learning curve is real but short (2 weeks of active posting).

### LinkedIn

- **Strengths:** High engagement rates for professional content. Long shelf life (posts surface for days). Good for B2B and enterprise-adjacent products. Comment sections can drive genuine business conversations.
- **Weaknesses:** Cringe factor is high. "Agree?" and humble-brag culture. Algorithm rewards personal stories over product content. Many technical audiences avoid it.
- **Best for:** B2B tools, productivity software, enterprise features, professional services, hiring/team-building tools.
- **Content that works:** Personal stories tied to business insights. Specific results with numbers. Hot takes on industry trends. "We changed X and here's what happened."
- **Founder capability note:** You need a personal profile with a real photo and real connections. Company pages get almost no organic reach. This channel requires you personally.

### Reddit

- **Strengths:** Extremely targeted subreddits exist for almost every niche. High trust in authentic content. Posts can surface for months via search. Community feedback is brutally honest (which is valuable).
- **Weaknesses:** Self-promotion is punished severely. Each subreddit has different rules. You need to be a genuine community member first. One wrong post can get you banned.
- **Best for:** Niche technical tools, products that solve a specific community pain, anything where the ICP has an active subreddit.
- **Content that works:** Genuine "I built this to solve my own problem" posts. Detailed technical write-ups. Asking for feedback (authentically). Answering questions where your product is relevant (without being salesy).
- **Founder capability note:** You must already be a Reddit user who understands the culture. If you don't have karma and history, start participating 2-4 weeks before posting about your product.

### Hacker News / Show HN

- **Strengths:** Massive reach if you hit the front page. Technical audience that values substance. Show HN is explicitly designed for launching. Strong SEO (HN posts rank well).
- **Weaknesses:** Binary outcome (front page or invisible). Audience is opinionated and will find every flaw. Comments can be harsh. You get one Show HN shot per product version.
- **Best for:** Developer tools, open source projects, technical products with a clear demo, anything with a good "how we built this" story.
- **Content that works:** Show HN with a clear one-line description and a demo link. Blog posts about technical challenges you solved. Open source announcements with substance.
- **Founder capability note:** Read HN daily for a week before posting. Understand what gets upvoted. Your Show HN post should lead with what the product does, not who you are.

### SEO / Blog

- **Strengths:** Compounds over time. Once ranked, a post drives traffic for months or years. High-intent visitors (they searched for this). Establishes expertise and trust.
- **Weaknesses:** Slow. 3-6 months to see results. Requires consistent publishing. Competitive keywords may be unreachable for a new site. Technical SEO setup takes time.
- **Best for:** Products that solve searchable problems. Anything where the ICP googles their problem before looking for solutions. Educational content, comparisons, how-to guides.
- **Content that works:** "How to [solve specific problem]" guides. Comparison posts ("[your product] vs [alternative]"). Technical deep-dives. Problem-first content that mentions your product as one solution.
- **Founder capability note:** You need basic SEO knowledge (keyword research, meta tags, internal linking). If you don't have a blog set up, add 1-2 weeks to the timeline. Consider starting with platforms (Dev.to, Hashnode) while building your own blog.

### Email / Newsletter

- **Strengths:** You own the channel. No algorithm changes. Direct relationship with subscribers. Highest conversion rates of any channel for warm audiences. Segmentation allows targeted messaging.
- **Weaknesses:** You need subscribers first (chicken-and-egg). Deliverability is a skill. Cold email has legal constraints (CAN-SPAM, GDPR). Building a list takes time.
- **Best for:** Products with a clear upgrade path. SaaS with trial-to-paid conversion. Products that benefit from education/onboarding. Anything where the sale requires multiple touches.
- **Content that works:** Welcome sequences that educate and qualify. Weekly insights related to the problem you solve. Case studies and results. Product updates tied to user benefits.
- **Founder capability note:** Setting up email infra (Resend, ConvertKit, Buttondown) takes a few hours. Writing good email copy is a learnable skill. The hard part is getting your first 100 subscribers.

### YouTube / Video

- **Strengths:** Extremely high trust (people see your face/voice). Long shelf life (YouTube is a search engine). Compounds over time. Great for products that benefit from demos.
- **Weaknesses:** High production cost per piece. Editing takes time. Requires on-camera comfort. Slow to build an audience. Algorithm favors consistency (weekly uploads).
- **Best for:** Visual products, developer tools with impressive demos, educational content, "build with me" series, products where seeing is believing.
- **Content that works:** 5-10 minute demos. "I built X in Y minutes" timelapses. Tutorial series. Comparison videos. Conference talks repurposed.
- **Founder capability note:** If you're comfortable on camera and can edit video (even basic cuts), score 6+. If not, consider starting with screen recordings and voiceover. Shorts/Reels can work with lower production quality.

### Product Hunt

- **Strengths:** Built specifically for product launches. Engaged early-adopter audience. Social proof (badges, rankings). PR pickup from successful launches. Good backlink for SEO.
- **Weaknesses:** One-shot event, not a channel. Traffic spike is temporary (1-3 days). Many upvotes come from other makers, not your ICP. Hunter/maker ecosystem can feel performative.
- **Best for:** Consumer-facing products. Developer tools with a polished landing page. Products with a visual wow factor. V1 launches and major version releases.
- **Content that works:** Clear tagline, good screenshots/video, maker comments that tell the backstory. Ship it on a Tuesday or Wednesday for best results.
- **Founder capability note:** Product Hunt is not a marketing channel, it's a launch event. Plan it as a one-day campaign, not an ongoing strategy. Have your other channels ready to capture the traffic spike.

## Decision Framework

After scoring, use this framework:

1. **Top 2 channels by composite score** become your focus
2. **If scores are close** (within 20%), pick the one with higher Time-to-Signal to learn faster
3. **If your top channel is slow** (SEO, YouTube), pair it with a fast channel (X, Reddit) so you get signal while the slow channel builds
4. **Re-evaluate after 2 weeks** of consistent effort. If the channel isn't showing signal by then (and its Time-to-Signal score predicted you'd have data by now), pivot to channel #3
5. **Never run more than 2 channels simultaneously** until one is clearly working. Splitting effort across 5 channels means doing all of them badly.
