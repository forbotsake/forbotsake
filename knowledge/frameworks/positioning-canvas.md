# Positioning Canvas

Reference framework for crafting a positioning statement that actually differentiates.

## The Positioning Statement Template

```
For [ICP - specific person, not category],
[product] is the [category]
that [key benefit - what changes for them]
unlike [current alternative - what they do today],
which [limitation of the alternative].
```

Every slot must be filled with something specific. If any slot contains a generic phrase ("businesses," "saves time," "is better"), the positioning is not done.

### Example: Good

```
For backend engineers at Series A startups who maintain internal APIs,
DocGenX is the API documentation tool
that generates accurate docs from code comments in 30 seconds
unlike Swagger/OpenAPI manual annotation,
which takes 2-3 hours per endpoint and drifts out of date within a week.
```

Why this works:
- "Backend engineers at Series A startups who maintain internal APIs" is a person you can find
- "30 seconds" is specific and verifiable
- "2-3 hours per endpoint" makes the pain concrete
- "Drifts out of date within a week" names the real frustration

### Example: Bad

```
For businesses,
our platform is a productivity solution
that helps teams work better
unlike other tools,
which are less efficient.
```

Why this fails:
- "Businesses" is everyone (so it's no one)
- "Productivity solution" is a category so broad it's meaningless
- "Helps teams work better" says nothing specific
- "Other tools" names no real alternative
- "Less efficient" is the most generic complaint possible

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### 1. Too Broad

**Symptom:** Your ICP is "developers" or "startups" or "small businesses."

**Test:** Can you find this person's LinkedIn profile? If your ICP description matches 10 million people, it's too broad. If it matches 10,000 people, you're getting closer. If you can name 10 specific individuals, you're there.

**Fix:** Keep narrowing. "Developers" becomes "frontend developers" becomes "React developers at e-commerce companies" becomes "React developers at Shopify Plus stores who build custom checkout flows." Now you know where to find them, what to say, and what they care about.

### 2. Feature-Focused Instead of Outcome-Focused

**Symptom:** Your positioning leads with what the product does ("AI-powered," "cloud-native," "real-time") instead of what changes for the customer.

**Test:** Read your positioning statement out loud. Does it describe a technology or a transformation? Your ICP doesn't care about your architecture. They care about their problem being solved.

**Fix:** For every feature, ask "so what?" three times.
- "We use AI to analyze code." So what?
- "It finds bugs faster." So what?
- "You ship on Friday without the Sunday morning panic." THAT is the positioning.

### 3. No Real Differentiation

**Symptom:** Your "unlike" clause could apply to any competitor. Or worse, you skip the "unlike" entirely because you think the product "speaks for itself."

**Test:** Replace your product name with a competitor's name. Does the positioning statement still make sense? If yes, you haven't differentiated.

**Fix:** Find the one thing you do that no alternative does. Not "better" — different. Maybe it's the approach (code-first vs config-first). Maybe it's the constraint you embrace (no cloud, local only). Maybe it's who you built it for (solo developers, not enterprise teams). Difference beats "better" every time because "better" is an opinion and "different" is a fact.

### 4. Aspirational Instead of Current

**Symptom:** Your positioning describes what the product will do in 6 months, not what it does today.

**Test:** Can a user experience the key benefit in the first 10 minutes? If the benefit requires features you haven't built, you're positioning a roadmap, not a product.

**Fix:** Position what exists. If what exists isn't compelling enough to position, that's a product problem, not a marketing problem.

### 5. Trying to Be Everything

**Symptom:** Three or more "and" clauses in your positioning. "We do X and Y and Z for A and B and C."

**Test:** Can you explain what you do in under 10 words? If not, you're trying to be too many things.

**Fix:** Pick one. The best positioning sacrifices breadth for clarity. You can expand later. Right now, be the obvious choice for one specific problem.

## The "Would Your ICP Forward This?" Test

The ultimate positioning test is not whether your ICP would read your messaging. It's whether they would forward it to a colleague.

### How to Apply

Take your positioning statement and imagine sending it as a message to your ICP. Ask:

1. **Would they read past the first line?** If the hook doesn't grab them in the context of where they'll see it (timeline, inbox, search results), the rest doesn't matter.

2. **Would they think "this is about me"?** Not "this could apply to me." The reaction should be "how do they know my exact situation?"

3. **Would they forward it?** This is the real test. Forwarding means: "I trust this enough to attach my reputation to it, AND I know someone else who needs to see this." If the positioning is too generic, there's no one specific to forward it to. If it's too self-promotional, they won't risk their credibility.

### What Makes Content Forwardable

- **Specificity:** "This tool cuts API doc time from 3 hours to 30 seconds" forwards. "This tool improves documentation productivity" doesn't.
- **Social proof by proxy:** When someone forwards your content, they're saying "I'm the kind of person who knows about tools like this." Make that identity attractive.
- **Utility:** The recipient should benefit from receiving the forward. Not "look at this cool startup" but "you were complaining about this exact problem last week."

### Red Flags (Not Forwardable)

- Contains the word "synergy," "leverage," "empower," or "revolutionize"
- Reads like it was written by a committee or a language model on default settings
- Makes claims without specifics ("10x faster" without faster than what, at what task)
- Focuses on the company/founder instead of the reader's problem
- Could describe any of 50 products in the same space
